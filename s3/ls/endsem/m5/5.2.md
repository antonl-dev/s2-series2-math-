# **Module 5: Responsibilities & Rights**

## **Topic 5.2: Collective bargaining - Confidentiality - Role of confidentiality in moral integrity**

This section examines two distinct but important concepts in professional life: Collective Bargaining, which deals with the power dynamics between employees and employers, and Confidentiality, which is a cornerstone of professional trust.

### **1. Collective Bargaining**

*   **Definition:** "It is the bargain by the trade union for improving the economic interests of the worker members." It is a process of negotiation between employees (usually represented by a trade union) and employers. The process may include negotiation, verbal threats, and the declaration of a 'strike'.
*   **The Core Conflict: Professionalism vs. Unionism:**
    There is an inherent tension between the principles of professionalism and the methods of unionism.
    *   **Professional Societies' View:** Professional societies like the NSPE often refuse to accept "collective coercive action." They hold that professionals should not promote their own interests at the expense of the dignity and integrity of the profession. They see the engineer's primary role as a **"faithful agent or trustee"** of their employer.
    *   **Unionism View:** Unions argue that collective bargaining is necessary to protect employees from exploitation, ensure fair wages and safe working conditions, and provide a balance of power against management.

*   **Ethical Considerations of Collective Bargaining:**
    The textbook presents a balanced view, showing arguments both for and against unionism.

    | **For Unionism (Pro-Union Arguments)**                                                                         | **Against Unionism (Anti-Union Arguments)**                                                                          |
    | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
    | 1. Improves standard of living and economic benefits for workers.                                             | 1. Can lead to inflationary conditions by driving up wages and expenses.                                            |
    | 2. Secures greater participation in management and gives workers a voice.                                     | 2. Can be destructive and negative, leading to loss of man-days through strikes. Individual worker opinions are suppressed. |

### **2. Confidentiality**

*   **Definition:** "Confidentiality means keeping the information on the employer and clients, as secrets." It is one of the most important aspects of professional work, especially in a team environment.
*   **Types of Confidential Information:**
    1.  **Privileged Information:** Information that is available only on the basis of a special privilege (e.g., a "need-to-know" basis). This includes information on projects that an engineer is specifically assigned to.
    2.  **Proprietary Information:** Information that the company owns, often as a trade secret. It refers to the company's knowledge and procedures that are not to be shared with outsiders. The trade secret is not legally protected unless it is registered, but it is an ethical obligation for the employee to keep it confidential.
    
    *   **Level of Confidentiality:**
        *   **Obvious Confidentiality:** Information about test results, new product designs, etc., where the loss from a breach would be severe.
        *   **Lesser Confidentiality:** Business information like the number of employees, supplier lists, etc., where the loss is less severe but still significant.

### **3. The Role of Confidentiality and its Justification**

Confidentiality is a cornerstone of professional ethics, and its importance is justified by several ethical theories and moral principles.

#### **Justification through Ethical Theories:**

*   **Rights-Based Theory:** This theory justifies confidentiality by emphasizing the right of stakeholders to protect their intellectual property and the right of the company to maintain its competitive advantage.
*   **Duty-Based Theory:** This theory emphasizes that employees have a duty to keep their employer's information secret as part of their role as a "faithful agent." It is a duty based on maintaining mutual trust.
*   **Utilitarian Theory:** This theory justifies confidentiality when it produces the most good for the most people. For example, keeping a new product design secret allows the company to succeed, which in turn provides jobs for employees and benefits for society.

#### **Justification through Moral Principles:**

1.  **Respect for Autonomy:** Confidentiality respects the freedom and self-determination of individuals and organizations to control their own personal or proprietary information. Without this control, they cannot protect their privacy and self-interest.
2.  **Respect for Promises:** Confidentiality is often part of an explicit or implicit promise (e.g., in an employment contract) made between employers and employees. Breaking confidentiality is breaking a promise.
3.  **Respect for Public Welfare:** In some cases, confidentiality is crucial for public welfare. For example, if a medical practitioner keeps a patient's diagnosis confidential, it encourages the patient to be honest and receive proper treatment. Similarly, a lawyer keeping client information confidential ensures a fair legal process.

#### **Role of Confidentiality in Moral Integrity:**

*   **Definition of Moral Integrity:** Moral integrity is the consistency of one's attitudes, emotions, and conduct in relation to justified moral values.
*   **The Connection:** The role of confidentiality in moral integrity is to act as a **test of that consistency**. A professional with moral integrity will uphold their duty of confidentiality even when it is difficult or when there is a personal temptation to do otherwise (e.g., using confidential information for personal gain). By keeping secrets, the professional demonstrates that their actions are consistently aligned with their duties of loyalty and trustworthiness. Breaking confidentiality, therefore, is a direct breach of one's moral integrity.

**2. Explain the importance of confidentiality in ethical business practices.**
*(Source: Third Semester B. Tech. Degree Examination, November 2024, Q 10)*

**Answer:**
Confidentiality is critically important in ethical business practices for several reasons:
1.  **Protects Intellectual Property:** It safeguards a company's trade secrets, new product designs, and proprietary processes, which are often its most valuable assets and the source of its competitive advantage.
2.  **Builds Trust with Clients:** Clients share sensitive information with businesses (e.g., financial data, strategic plans). Maintaining confidentiality is essential for building and maintaining the client's trust. A breach of confidentiality can destroy a business relationship.
3.  **Ensures Fair Competition:** It prevents employees from using inside information for personal gain or giving an unfair advantage to a competitor (e.g., by leaking bid information or marketing plans).
4.  **Upholds Professional Integrity:** The act of maintaining confidentiality is a core tenet of professionalism. It demonstrates that an employee is trustworthy, loyal, and has high moral integrity, which enhances both their personal reputation and that of the business.

This knowledge is considered the intellectual property of the former employer, Company A. Using it for the benefit of a direct competitor is a serious ethical violation.
*   **Connection to Moral Integrity:** Moral integrity is the consistency between one's values and actions. A professional with integrity upholds their ethical duties even when no one is watching and even after a formal relationship (like employment) has ended. In this case, the engineer's action is inconsistent with their enduring professional duty of loyalty and trustworthiness. By using the secret knowledge, they are prioritizing their new employer's gain over their past ethical obligations, thus demonstrating a lack of moral integrity.
