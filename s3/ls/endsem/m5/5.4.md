# **Module 5: Responsibilities & Rights**

## **Topic 5.4: Professional rights - Employee right - IPR Discrimination**

This section focuses on the rights that individuals hold within the professional workplace. It distinguishes between the specific rights of professionals, the general rights of all employees, and touches upon the concept of Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) in this context.

### **1. Professional Rights**

Professional rights are those moral entitlements that are essential for professionals to fulfill their duties in an ethical and effective manner.

ðŸ§  **Mnemonic:** "**C**onscientious **R**eferees get **R**ecognition"
*   **C** - (Right of Professional) **C**onscience
*   **R** - (Right of Conscientious) **R**efusal
*   **R** - (Right to Fair) **R**ecognition

1.  **Right of Professional Conscience:**
    *   **Definition:** This is the right to form and express a professional judgment, based on one's expertise and moral values. It empowers professionals to exercise their own reasoned technical and ethical judgments in carrying out their work.
    *   **Importance:** This right is the foundation of professional autonomy and is necessary for an engineer to uphold their primary duty to public safety.

2.  **Right of Conscientious Refusal:**
    *   **Definition:** This is the right to refuse to participate in unethical activities. An employer cannot force an employee to do something that the employee reasonably views as unethical or illegal.
    *   **Examples of Activities that can be Refused:** Falsifying data, forging documents, altering test results, lying, or participating in projects that pose a clear and present danger to human life.
    *   **Importance:** This right protects a professional's moral integrity and serves as a critical check against unethical corporate behavior.

3.  **Right to Fair Recognition and Remuneration:**
    *   **Definition:** This is the right to receive fair credit and compensation for one's professional work and achievements.
    *   **Importance:** This right is related to both morality and self-interest. Fair recognition (both monetary and non-monetary) motivates professionals to excel in their work and to continue their professional development. It prevents situations where engineers who contribute to valuable patents or innovations are not adequately rewarded by their organizations.

### **2. Employee Rights**

While professional rights are specific to professionals, employee rights are the moral and legal rights that apply to all individuals in the status of being an employee.

ðŸ§  **Mnemonic:** "**P**eople **N**eed **D**ue **E**quality"
*   **P** - (Right to) **P**rivacy
*   **N** - (Right to) **N**on-discrimination
*   **D** - (Right to) **D**ue Process
*   **E** - (Right to) **E**qual Opportunity

1.  **Right to Privacy:**
    *   **Definition:** The right to control access to and use of personal information about oneself. This also includes the right to a private life outside of the job.
    *   **Limitations:** This right is not absolute. It can be limited when an employee's outside activities form a conflict of interest or damage the employer's interests. Employers also have a right to monitor activities that occur on company property or using company resources.

2.  **Right to Non-discrimination:**
    *   **Definition:** The right to be treated fairly and not be subjected to morally unjust treatment on the basis of characteristics like race, sex, religion, creed, or language. Discrimination is damaging to human dignity.

3.  **Right to Due Process:**
    *   **Definition:** This is the right to a fair process in any disciplinary action, such as firing, demotion, or reprimand.
    *   **Requirements:** This typically means the employee has a right to be informed of the charges against them, to have a fair hearing, and to have the decision given in writing with clearly stated reasons. Fairness here is about the *process*, not necessarily the *outcome*.

4.  **Right to Equal Opportunity:**
    *   **Definition:** The right to be judged on the basis of one's qualifications and performance, not on irrelevant characteristics. This can sometimes be supported by **Affirmative Action** (or preferential treatment), which aims to give preference to groups that were denied equal treatment in the past to reverse historical discrimination.

### **3. Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) and Discrimination**

*   **IPR Context:** While IPR (Patents, Copyrights, Trademarks) are covered in more detail elsewhere, in this context they relate to the "Right to Fair Recognition." An engineer or employee who creates a valuable piece of intellectual property (like a new invention or software) as part of their job has a right to be fairly compensated and recognized for it, according to their employment agreement and the law.
*   **Discrimination in IPR:** The term "IPR Discrimination" as listed in the syllabus likely refers to discriminatory practices related to the recognition or reward for intellectual property. For example, if a company systematically gives more credit or financial rewards for patents to employees of a certain gender or nationality, while downplaying the contributions of others, it would be a form of discrimination in the context of IPR. It would be a violation of both the Right to Fair Recognition and the Right to Non-discrimination.

### **Practice Questions**

**1. List the features of the employee rights.**
*(Source: Question Bank, Q 4.40)*

**Answer:**
The key features of employee rights include:
1.  **Right to Privacy:** The right to control personal information and have a private life outside of work.
2.  **Right to Non-discrimination:** The right to be treated fairly without prejudice based on race, gender, religion, etc.
3.  **Right to Due Process:** The right to a fair and transparent procedure during any disciplinary action from the employer.
4.  **Right to Equal Opportunity:** The right to be considered for jobs and promotions based on merit and qualifications, not irrelevant factors. This can include affirmative action policies.

**2. What is meant by 'right of conscientious refusal'?**
*(Source: Question Bank, Q 4.39)*

**Answer:**
The right of conscientious refusal is the right of an employee or professional to refuse to participate in an activity that they believe is unethical or illegal. An employer cannot force an employee to perform an act that violates their moral conscience, such as falsifying data, lying to a client, forging documents, or designing an unsafe product. This right is a critical protection for a professional's moral integrity and serves as an important ethical safeguard within an organization.

**3. ðŸ¤– An engineer is asked by her manager to sign off on a report containing data she knows to be falsified to make a product look safer than it is. Which two professional rights are most directly relevant to this situation, and what should she do?**

**Answer:**
The two most relevant professional rights are:
1.  **The Right of Professional Conscience:** This is her right to exercise her own professional and moral judgment. Her judgment is that the report is false and the product is unsafe.
2.  **The Right of Conscientious Refusal:** This is her right to refuse to participate in an unethical act, which in this case is signing a fraudulent document.

**What she should do:**
She should exercise her right of conscientious refusal. She should refuse to sign the report and, using her right of professional conscience, she should clearly and professionally state her reasons to her manager, explaining that the data is false and that signing it would be a violation of her professional duty to protect the public. If the manager persists, she has an obligation to escalate the issue to higher management or the company's ethics department.
