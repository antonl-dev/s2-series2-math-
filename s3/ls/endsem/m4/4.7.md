# **Module 4: Human Values**

## **Topic 4.7: Code of Conduct in Engineering profession**

A Code of Conduct, also known as a Code of Ethics, is a formal statement of the values and ethical principles that a profession or organization is committed to upholding. For the engineering profession, these codes serve as a practical guide for making decisions that align with the core value of protecting the public.

### **1. What is a Code of Conduct?**

*   **Definition:** "The â€˜codes of ethicsâ€™ exhibit, rights, duties, and obligations of the members of a profession and a professional society." They are a set of rules and guidelines that define expected standards of behavior for professionals.
*   **Purpose:** They translate broad ethical principles (like honesty, integrity) into specific, actionable guidelines relevant to the profession.

### **2. The Essential Roles of an Engineering Code of Conduct**

Engineering codes of ethics are not just decorative documents; they serve several critical functions:

1.  **Inspiration and Guidance:** The codes express the collective commitment of the profession to ethical conduct and serving the public good. They provide a positive vision that can inspire individual engineers and offer clear guidance when they face ethical dilemmas.

2.  **Support to Engineers:** The codes give positive support to professionals who are taking a stand on a moral issue. If an engineer is pressured by a manager to compromise on safety, they can point to the code of ethics as a justification for their refusal. The code can also serve as a potential legal support in such cases.

3.  **Deterrence and Discipline:** The codes serve as a basis for investigating unethical actions. Professional societies can suspend or expel members who are proven to have violated the code. This sanction, along with the loss of respect from peers, acts as a deterrent against unethical behavior.

4.  **Education and Mutual Understanding:** Codes prompt discussion and reflection on moral issues. They help create a shared understanding among professionals, the public, and the government about the moral responsibilities of engineers.

5.  **Creating a Good Public Image:** A strong code of ethics presents a positive image of a committed and trustworthy profession to the public. This promotes self-regulation and can reduce the need for restrictive government regulations, as it builds public trust.

6.  **Protecting the Status Quo (in a positive sense):** They create a minimum level of ethical conduct that all members of the profession are expected to adhere to. The primary obligation to public safety, declared in all major codes, serves to protect the public.

7.  **Promoting Business Interests:** By establishing shared standards, the codes can promote healthy competition and ensure a level playing field, which ultimately benefits investors, employees, and consumers.

### **3. The Fundamental Canons (Principles) of Engineering Ethics**

While different professional societies (like the NSPE, IEI, IEEE) have their own specific wording, their codes of ethics are built upon a set of shared fundamental principles, often called "canons."

A typical and widely accepted set of canons, based on the National Society of Professional Engineers (NSPE) code, is:

**Engineers, in the fulfillment of their professional duties, shall:**

1.  **Hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public.**
    *   This is the **primary directive** of the engineering profession. If an engineer's judgment is overruled in a way that endangers the public, they have an obligation to notify the proper authorities. This canon overrides all other obligations.

2.  **Perform services only in areas of their competence.**
    *   Engineers must not take on work that they are not qualified to perform by education or experience. This is a commitment to professional competence.

3.  **Issue public statements only in an objective and truthful manner.**
    *   This canon is about honesty and transparency. Engineers must be truthful in their professional reports and testimony and must include all relevant information.

4.  **Act for each employer or client as faithful agents or trustees.**
    *   This is the duty of loyalty. Engineers must avoid conflicts of interest and must not disclose confidential information without the consent of their client or employer.

5.  **Avoid deceptive acts.**
    *   This is a commitment to honesty. Engineers shall not falsify their qualifications or misrepresent their experience.

6.  **Conduct themselves honorably, responsibly, ethically, and lawfully so as to enhance the honor, reputation, and usefulness of the profession.**
    *   This is the duty to uphold the integrity of the profession itself, acting in a way that brings credit to all engineers.

### **Practice Questions**

**1. Explain the positive and negative features of code of ethics.**
*(Source: Third Semester B. Tech. Degree Examination, November 2024, Q 17)*

**Answer:**
**Positive Features (Roles):**
*   **Guidance and Inspiration:** Provides a clear guide for ethical decision-making and inspires professionals to uphold the values of their profession.
*   **Support for Professionals:** Offers a formal basis for engineers to refuse unethical requests from employers, giving them institutional backing.
*   **Deterrence and Discipline:** Discourages unethical behavior through the threat of sanctions (like loss of membership) and provides a framework for disciplinary action.
*   **Public Trust:** Builds a positive public image of the profession as trustworthy and committed to public welfare.
*   **Education:** Promotes a shared understanding of ethical responsibilities among professionals and the public.

**Negative Features (Limitations):**
*   **Vague Wordings:** Many codes are written in general terms and may not provide clear answers to specific, complex ethical dilemmas.
*   **Internal Conflicts:** Different clauses within a code can sometimes conflict. For example, the duty of loyalty to a client might conflict with the duty to protect the public if the client insists on an unsafe design.
*   **Not a Final Authority:** Codes are not laws. They have flaws and cannot cover every possible situation. They are guidelines, not absolute rules.
*   **Limited Enforcement:** Professional societies have limited enforcement power. They can only discipline their own members, and many practicing engineers are not members.
*   **Coercive Appearance:** Some view the codes as coercive or threatening, potentially stifling dissent rather than guiding it.

The code of ethics, which states they must "hold paramount the safety of the public," gives them the formal justification and moral support to refuse the manager's request. It transforms a personal opinion into a professional obligation. Following the code protects the public, and in the long run, protects the engineer's own professional reputation and integrity.

*(Source: Internal Assessment (Series Test-II), October - 2025, Q 11 a)*

**Answer:**
According to a standard engineering Code of Conduct, the engineer has a clear set of obligations in this situation, primarily dictated by the first and most important canon:

1.  **Hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public.**
    *   The use of substandard materials in a project (e.g., a building, a bridge) directly threatens public safety. This means the engineer's primary duty is to address this threat.

2.  **Act as a faithful agent or trustee to the employer/client.**
    *   This means the engineer's first step should be to report their findings internally.

**The engineer should take the following steps:**
1.  **Report Internally:** The engineer must immediately and formally report their findings to their direct supervisor or the relevant project manager, explaining the specific materials that are substandard and the potential safety risks involved. This fulfills their duty of loyalty.
2.  **Escalate if Necessary:** If their immediate supervisor ignores the concern or is complicit, the engineer has an ethical obligation to escalate the issue up the chain of command within the organization.
3.  **Notify Appropriate Authorities (If necessary):** If the organization refuses to correct the situation, the engineer's paramount duty to the public overrides their duty to the employer. At this point, they may be obligated to report the violation to the appropriate external authorities, such as a government regulatory body or a professional engineering society. This act is often referred to as "whistleblowing."
